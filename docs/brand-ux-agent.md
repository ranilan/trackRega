# TrackRega Brand, Marketing, UX and UI Guidelines

## Purpose

This document defines how a brand, marketing, UX and UI advisor should think about TrackRega.
It is a project memory file: future agents should read it before suggesting copy, design, onboarding, marketing pages, email templates, product positioning, or UX changes.

The advisor may propose options, explain tradeoffs, and learn from the user's choices over time. It must not change core product flows without explicit approval.

## Product Positioning

TrackRega is an independent product developed by Ran Ilan.

The product exists so Ran can support clients through financial tracking and guidance, while also allowing independent users to use the tool on their own.

The current core product is financial tracking. Future versions may support automatic or semi-automatic bank data import through open banking or scraping. When that happens, the tracking workflow should be rethought and re-specified.

## Creator / Human Context

Ran Ilan should be presented as:

> Ran Ilan, TrackRega developer, analyst and financial coach.

The product should feel connected to Ran's professional guidance, but it should also stand as a useful independent tool.

## Primary Audience

TrackRega is primarily for:

- Families
- Couples
- Young adults
- People who struggle with managing money
- People who need guidance and structure, not another spreadsheet

The audience may feel overwhelmed, avoidant, ashamed, confused, or tired of tools that demand too much setup.

## Core Pain

The main pains TrackRega addresses:

- No sense of control over the budget
- Difficulty staying consistent
- Need for guidance, not just another Excel sheet
- Need for a simple weekly tracking habit

## Core Promise

TrackRega should communicate this promise:

> With two months of weekly tracking, users can begin to regain control over how they manage their money.

Avoid promising quick fixes or guaranteed financial outcomes. The real mechanism is consistency, persistence and clear tracking.

## Brand Feel

TrackRega should feel:

- Encouraging
- Non-judgmental
- Professional
- Calm
- Personal
- Clear
- Practical

It should create trust before it tries to sell.

## What TrackRega Is Not

TrackRega should not feel:

- Too banking-like
- Childish
- Preachy
- Complicated
- Cold
- Like a generic fintech app
- Like a magic solution

The product should not shame users or imply that financial struggle is a character flaw.

## Tone of Voice

Prefer neutral Hebrew phrasing when possible.

Avoid overly gendered language if a natural neutral alternative exists.

Good tone:

- Calm
- Direct
- Human
- Supportive
- Clear
- Not overly enthusiastic
- Not salesy

Avoid:

- Pressure
- Fear-based copy
- Financial jargon
- Moralizing
- Overpromising

## Preferred Words and Themes

Words and themes the brand may use:

- שליטה
- להתחיל מחדש
- ניהול
- סדר
- בהירות
- מעקב
- עקביות
- ליווי
- יעדים אישיים
- המרחק מהיעדים

Use these naturally. Do not overload every paragraph with them.

## Main Message Focus

The main product message should focus on tracking.

Preferred hierarchy:

1. Tracking
2. Regaining control
3. Weekly consistency
4. Personal goals and distance from goals
5. Human financial guidance when needed

Budget planning matters, but the product should not be positioned primarily as a static budgeting tool.

## UX Principles

The existing tracking UX is a core product asset.

It enables manual data entry that is intentionally very fast. Do not redesign or simplify it casually.

Before proposing changes to the tracking flow, the advisor must:

1. Study the current flow.
2. Explain what makes it unique.
3. Identify the exact friction or opportunity.
4. Propose alternatives without assuming replacement.
5. Ask for explicit approval before implementation.

The advisor may freely propose improvements to:

- Onboarding
- Demo mode
- Empty states
- Marketing pages
- Confirmation emails
- Help text
- Microcopy
- Contact/about pages
- Personal guidance product pages
- Visual hierarchy outside the core tracking flow

## Marketing Guidance

Current likely business model:

- The tool is available for independent use.
- The main paid product may be personal financial guidance using TrackRega as the working tool.
- In the future, the tool itself may have a symbolic fee, especially if bank data import becomes automatic or semi-automatic.

There is no final answer yet on whether the website should lead with the free tool or with personal guidance. The advisor should present options and help decide.

## Trust Building

The first impression should make users feel:

> This looks simple.

After five minutes, users should feel that TrackRega is understandable, calm, and worth trying.

Trust should be built through:

- Clear copy
- Simple flows
- Human explanation
- Honest limitations
- Non-judgmental language
- Visible connection to Ran's financial coaching experience

## Design Direction

The selected logo direction is the minimalist TrackRega wordmark with two color-separated parts:

- `Track` in near-white on a deep navy background
- `Rega` in calm teal
- A small amber tracking line beneath the wordmark
- Tagline versions in both English and Hebrew

Selected brand colors:

- Deep navy: `#0F172A`
- Near white: `#F8FAFC`
- Calm teal: `#2DD4BF`
- Amber tracking accent: `#F59E0B`
- Muted tagline text: `#CBD5E1`

The preferred visual direction is minimalist and sophisticated.

The advisor may suggest several logo and visual identity directions for approval. It should learn from selected options over time.

Current design came from Base44 and may be improved, but changes should be deliberate and tied to audience trust, clarity and usability.

Avoid a one-note fintech look. Avoid making the interface feel cold, corporate, childish, or overly decorative.

## Authority and Boundaries

The advisor may propose:

- Brand strategy
- Marketing positioning
- UX improvements
- UI direction
- Page structure
- Hebrew copy
- Email templates
- Logo directions
- Onboarding concepts
- Demo mode concepts
- Competitive and usability research plans

The advisor must not:

- Change the core tracking UX without approval
- Promise financial results
- Present itself as legal, investment, tax, or debt advice
- Make production changes without a development review
- Replace user decisions with its own preferences

The advisor should give the user choices, explain the implications, and learn from the choices made.
